How to evaluate and use this workflow

  1. Define the hands-free moment. Start by identifying when Meta Display adds value: walking, commuting, cooking, or presenting. In these moments, typing is awkward but context matters. Describe the task end-state clearly—“reorder my usual lunch” or “pull yesterday’s competitor pricing”—so Super knows what completion looks like, not just what page to open.
  2. Run the task once with supervision. The first run is about correctness. Super operates a real computer, navigating the same sites you would. Watch the steps on a larger screen if possible, confirm logins or selections, and correct small mistakes. This successful trace is what seeds the computer-use cache.
  3. Reuse via Meta Display prompts. On subsequent days, invoke the same workflow from Meta Display with a short prompt. Because Super can reuse the computer-use cache, it doesn’t need to rediscover every click. This is where wearables shine: brief voice commands trigger substantial background work.
  4. Add guardrails and confirmations. Decide which steps require explicit approval. For purchases or messages, keep a confirmation checkpoint that pings your display. This balances speed with safety, especially in public or mobile contexts.
  5. Expand gradually. Once one workflow is stable, layer adjacent tasks—saving receipts, sending summaries, or scheduling follow-ups. The goal is a small library of repeatable actions you can trigger from your glasses without cognitive load.

Implementation checklist

  • Meta Display pairing verified. Ensure your glasses, phone, and Super account are signed in consistently. Mismatched accounts break routing and make hands-free triggers unreliable during real-world use.
  • One workflow, one outcome. Resist the urge to bundle too much at once. Clear, single-purpose workflows are easier to confirm on a display and lead to cleaner cache reuse.
  • Confirmation language written. Predefine how Super should ask for approval—short, glanceable prompts that fit the Meta Display form factor without forcing long reads.
  • Error fallback defined. Decide what happens if a site layout changes or a login expires. A simple rule—pause and notify—prevents silent failures.
  • Privacy boundaries reviewed. Use Super’s approach of operating its own apps and sessions where possible so you’re not over-granting personal email or calendar access.
  • Repeat cadence tested. Run the workflow multiple times in a week to confirm the computer-use cache actually reduces friction and doesn’t drift from your intent.

Risks and limits

Interface drift. Websites change. Even with a computer-use cache, major UI updates can break steps. Plan for periodic supervised runs to refresh the workflow.

Security trade-offs. As Search Engine Journal notes, computer-controlling agents attract attackers. Limit scope, avoid over-broad permissions, and keep confirmation points for sensitive actions.

Wearable constraints. Meta Display is glanceable, not immersive. Long decision trees or dense information still belong on a larger screen.

Expectation mismatch. Compared to Siri, ChatGPT, Gemini, Grok, Folk, or Orchids, Super is not trying to be everything. It’s optimized for repeated computer work; one-off trivia questions may be better served elsewhere.
